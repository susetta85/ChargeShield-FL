# Critical Review of ChargeShield-FL Project Documentation
### DSN 2027 Program Committee Perspective

**Reviewer:** External Academic Reviewer (Simulated DSN 2027 Program Committee Perspective)
**Date:** 2026-06-26
**Subject:** ChargeShield-FL — Federated Learning Privacy Evaluation Framework for EV Charging Infrastructure

---

## 1. Executive Summary

The ChargeShield-FL project represents a technically ambitious effort to construct a rigorous, modular, and reproducible research framework for evaluating Membership Inference Attacks (MIA) against Federated Learning (FL) systems deployed within Operational Technology (OT) infrastructure—specifically Electric Vehicle (EV) charging networks. The documentation corpus reviewed for this assessment spans eleven documents: Architecture.md, ThreatModel.md, MLPlane.md, ProtocolAdapters.md, IDS.md, PrivacyAuditor.md, CaseStudies.md, DeveloperGuide.md, Testing.md, Roadmap.md, and README.md. Considered together, these documents reveal a project that is technically well-grounded at the implementation level but whose documentation fails, in several critical respects, to make the scientific case for the work with the clarity and rigor that a DSN 2027 submission would require.

The most significant strength of the project is the clarity of its architectural conception. The choice to deploy twelve nodes across four heterogeneous clusters—Highway, Urban, Residential, and Corporate—each running distinct protocol stacks (OCPP 1.6, OCPP 2.0.1, MQTT v5), and to federate learning over this topology using NVFLARE 2.7.2, constitutes a meaningfully realistic experimental platform. The use of the ACN-Data JPL dataset, comprising 13,073 real EV charging sessions from 2019 and 2020, further elevates the framework above simulation-only baselines. The initial experimental result, an AUC-ROC of 0.5172 for the FedMIA attack at ε=1.0, provides a first empirical signal that the Differential Privacy mechanism is functioning correctly and that the end-to-end pipeline is operationally sound. These are genuine contributions that merit academic presentation.

However, the documentation corpus as a whole suffers from a set of structural problems that would likely result in rejection or major revision requests from a DSN reviewer before a paper derived from this work could be accepted. The most pressing of these is the absence of any related work section or literature survey, either as a standalone document or embedded within the existing materials. Without a rigorous positioning of ChargeShield-FL against prior FL privacy evaluation frameworks, MIA literature, and OT/ICS security research, the novelty claim of the framework cannot be established. A DSN reviewer will not accept the claim that a framework is novel unless it is demonstrated to be so through comparison. Equally pressing is the incomplete experimental sweep: only the first case study has a single preliminary result, while Case Studies 2 and 3 have not been initiated.

The documentation also exhibits technical inconsistencies that undermine confidence in the implementation's correctness. The most consequential is the discrepancy between the epsilon estimation formula in PrivacyAuditor.md—which uses a simplified expression—and the exact Gaussian Mechanism formula implemented in GradientManager, where σ is computed as max_grad_norm × √(2×ln(1.25/δ)) / ε. If the auditing layer does not faithfully reflect the mechanism it is auditing, the privacy guarantees reported in the paper will be suspect. This inconsistency must be resolved before any empirical claims about epsilon are published. Additional inconsistencies in duplicated content between DeveloperGuide.md and Architecture.md, while less scientifically consequential, suggest that the documentation corpus lacks version discipline and coordinated editing.

The critical path toward a DSN 2027 submission is as follows. First, the experimental sweep—covering the full factorial of rounds ∈ {100, 200, 500, 1000} and ε ∈ {0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 5.0}—must be completed and the results subjected to rigorous statistical analysis, including confidence intervals and per-cluster breakdown. Second, a related work section of substantial depth must be written, positioning the framework against at minimum: Shokri et al. (2017), Nasr et al. (2019), McMahan et al. (2018), Blanchard et al. (2017), and the relevant OT security standards (IEC 62443, ISO 15118). Third, the threat model must be formalized, including an explicit attacker capability model and at minimum a STRIDE alignment. Fourth, the ML Plane must be reframed in the documentation as the novel architectural contribution it appears to be, rather than an implementation convenience. If these four tasks are completed with sufficient depth, the project has the substance to support a strong DSN 2027 submission.

## 2. Summary of Changes Required

### 2.1 Architecture.md

Architecture.md provides a competent overview of the ChargeShield-FL topology, including the four-cluster node layout and the network emulation stack, but it fails to contextualize the architecture within the OT domain with sufficient precision. The Purdue Model is referenced, and the ML Plane is described as crossing levels L0 through L3, but the trust boundaries between these levels are never formalized. A DSN reviewer will expect an explicit statement of which communication paths cross trust domains, what cryptographic mechanisms enforce those boundaries, and what the security implications of a compromise at each level would be. The current document treats the Purdue Model as a labeling convention rather than as a security architecture with formal trust assumptions.

The hardware constraints of EV charging infrastructure are not stated anywhere in the document. This is a significant omission because the argument for using FL in this context—rather than centralized learning—is partially grounded in the compute and bandwidth limitations of field-deployed charging nodes. If the Residential cluster nodes represent 7kW AC chargers with embedded controllers, the document should state what processor and memory budget is assumed, what this implies for model size and local training cost, and how this constrains the autoencoder architecture choices. The omission leaves the architectural motivation underspecified and vulnerable to reviewer challenge.

The ML Plane section within Architecture.md covers substantially the same content as MLPlane.md, creating redundancy without adding value. These two documents should be harmonized: Architecture.md should contain a brief, high-level description of the ML Plane as an architectural concept with a pointer to MLPlane.md for the full treatment, while MLPlane.md should be the authoritative reference. The current duplication risks divergence over time and already reflects inconsistencies in terminology. Additionally, the motivation for the specific protocol choices in each cluster—particularly the decision to run MQTT v5 in the Residential cluster rather than OCPP—should be explicitly argued in terms of real-world deployment practice, citing relevant industry documentation or prior literature.

### 2.2 ThreatModel.md

ThreatModel.md is among the weakest documents in the corpus from a scientific standpoint. The attacker taxonomy presented lacks the formal depth required for a systems security publication. Specifically, the document does not provide a formal capability model for the adversary: there is no explicit statement of what the attacker can observe (e.g., gradient updates only, or also global model parameters), what the attacker can control (e.g., a subset of clients, the aggregator), and what the attacker's computational budget is assumed to be. Without this formalization, the threat model cannot be precisely connected to the experimental setup, and a reviewer cannot verify that the FedMIA attack instantiates the stated threat model faithfully.

The trust assumptions in the document are stated informally, in prose, without any formal notation or reference to established threat modeling frameworks. For a DSN submission, the expected standard is either STRIDE (Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information Disclosure, Denial of Service, Elevation of Privilege) alignment for systems threats, or PASTA (Process for Attack Simulation and Threat Analysis) for a risk-oriented decomposition. The document should at minimum map each identified threat to the relevant STRIDE categories and demonstrate that the experimental scenarios cover a meaningful subset of the identified threat surface. The current document gives the impression that the threat model was written to justify the experiments already planned, rather than derived independently from first principles.

The attacker knowledge model is entirely absent. In the MIA literature, a fundamental distinction is drawn between black-box attackers (who can only query the model), white-box attackers (who have access to model parameters), and model-stealing attackers (who reconstruct the model). The FedMIA attack implemented here uses a shadow model approach, which implies specific knowledge assumptions about the training data distribution. These assumptions must be explicitly stated, compared to alternatives in the literature, and justified as realistic given the EV infrastructure deployment context. Without this, the experimental scenario is not interpretable by a reader unfamiliar with the internal project conventions.

### 2.3 MLPlane.md

MLPlane.md describes the ML Plane as a transversal logical layer that crosses the Purdue Model from L0 to L3 via an observer pattern, and this is the most genuinely novel architectural contribution in the entire framework. However, the document fails to make this novelty legible as a scientific contribution. The motivation for introducing this abstraction—specifically, the gap in the Purdue Model that the ML Plane addresses—is stated in a manner that a practitioner would find intuitive but that a reviewer would find insufficiently argued. The Purdue Model does not natively accommodate bidirectional, asynchronous data flows that carry model gradients alongside operational telemetry; this is the gap. The document should cite the relevant Purdue Model literature, articulate the gap formally, and then demonstrate how the ML Plane addresses it in a way that is generalizable beyond the EV charging context.

The observer pattern is described at the implementation level—as a design choice that decouples observation from computation—but it is not connected to the research motivation. The reason that observation decoupling matters scientifically is that it enables the same physical topology to be instrumented for different experimental conditions without modifying the core FL protocol. This is the property that makes the framework reproducible and extensible. The document should make this argument explicitly and connect it to the broader discourse on reproducibility in ML security research, where the inability to replicate experimental conditions is a known and documented problem.

The document should also address the question of whether the ML Plane abstraction is specific to the EV charging domain or whether it is a general contribution applicable to any FL-over-OT deployment. If it is general, this should be argued and the generality demonstrated through at least one additional hypothetical instantiation (e.g., smart grid metering, industrial process control). If it is domain-specific, the document should explain why, and what properties of EV charging infrastructure necessitate the specific design choices made. Currently, the document sits between these two framings without committing to either, which weakens the contribution narrative.

### 2.4 ProtocolAdapters.md

ProtocolAdapters.md is, as documented in this review, extremely thin—the most underdeveloped document in the corpus relative to the scientific importance of its subject matter. Protocol heterogeneity is a defining characteristic of real-world EV charging infrastructure, and the choice to support OCPP 1.6, OCPP 2.0.1, and MQTT v5 within a single framework is not a minor engineering convenience but a substantive research design decision. The document must be substantially expanded to justify each protocol choice, compare the protocols on dimensions relevant to the research (message structure, session semantics, security features, supported data granularity), and explain why the adapter pattern was selected as the integration architecture.

The comparison between OCPP 1.6 and OCPP 2.0.1 is particularly important and entirely absent from the current document. OCPP 2.0.1 introduces significant changes in security architecture, including mandatory TLS, CSMS-side certificate validation, and a restructured message format that affects the feature extraction pipeline. The document should articulate how the AutoencoderTrainer and feature extraction components handle the schema differences between protocol versions, and what the implications are for cross-cluster model aggregation when the input feature distributions may differ by protocol version. This is a non-trivial research question that the framework is positioned to address but does not yet acknowledge.

The adapter pattern itself—the design pattern used to abstract protocol differences behind a uniform interface—should be presented with its interface specification, its extension contract (what a developer must implement to add a new protocol), and its failure semantics (what happens when the underlying protocol connection is interrupted mid-session). The current document provides none of this. The document should also address whether the adapter pattern introduces any artifacts into the data that could confound the MIA experiments—for example, whether the translation from MQTT v5 payload structures to the six-feature ACN-Data schema introduces systematic biases that could affect reconstruction error distributions.

### 2.5 IDS.md

IDS.md describes three detection mechanisms—CUSUM statistical process control, Krum Byzantine-robust aggregation, and Cosine Similarity gradient comparison—but frames these components as features of the ChargeShield-FL system rather than as detection baselines against which the MIA scenarios are evaluated. This framing is scientifically misleading. CUSUM, Krum, and Cosine Similarity are well-established techniques; they appear in the literature as early as Blanchard et al. (2017) for Krum and in the statistical quality control literature for CUSUM. The document should acknowledge this explicitly and reframe the IDS components as a detection baseline layer whose purpose is to evaluate whether standard anomaly detection techniques can identify FL clients under MIA attack.

The absence of a negative result framing is a missed opportunity. If the IDS components fail to detect the FedMIA attack under certain conditions—for example, when the attacker operates below the CUSUM detection threshold—this is a scientifically interesting finding that should be anticipated in the IDS documentation and planned for reporting in the experimental results. A DSN reviewer will be skeptical of a framework that includes detection mechanisms but does not plan to report on their failure modes. The document should include a section on expected failure conditions for each detection mechanism and outline the experimental conditions under which each mechanism is expected to be ineffective.

The positioning of CUSUM, Krum, and Cosine Similarity relative to the current literature on Byzantine-robust FL and anomaly detection in federated systems is absent. The document should include at minimum a paragraph situating each mechanism within the relevant literature, explaining why these three mechanisms were selected over alternatives (e.g., FLTrust, Bulyan, norm-bounded clipping), and what properties of EV charging data make them more or less appropriate than alternatives. Without this positioning, the IDS layer appears ad hoc and the experimental comparisons that use it as a baseline will be difficult to interpret.

### 2.6 PrivacyAuditor.md

PrivacyAuditor.md contains a technical inconsistency of scientific consequence that must be resolved before any paper is submitted. The document uses a simplified formula for epsilon estimation that is not consistent with the exact Gaussian Mechanism formula implemented in GradientManager, where σ = max_grad_norm × √(2×ln(1.25/δ)) / ε. The privacy auditor is the component responsible for reporting the achieved privacy budget; if it does not correctly invert the noise mechanism to recover the epsilon value, then any epsilon reported in the experimental results will be inaccurate. The document must be rewritten to use the exact formula, and the implementation of PrivacyAuditor must be verified against GradientManager to ensure consistency.

The document does not engage with the composition of privacy budgets across FL rounds. When Differential Privacy (DP) is applied per-round with a fixed Gaussian Mechanism, the total privacy expenditure across T rounds is not simply T×ε under the standard composition theorem; advanced composition (Dwork et al., 2010) and Rényi Differential Privacy (Mironov, 2017) provide tighter bounds. The McMahan et al. (2018) DP-FedAvg paper uses the moments accountant precisely because the naive composition bound is excessively conservative. PrivacyAuditor.md must discuss this and state explicitly which composition analysis is being used, why, and what the implications are for the reported epsilon values. The absence of this discussion makes the DP claims in the framework non-comparable to those in the existing literature.

The document is also not connected to the broader DP theory in any rigorous way. It does not define the (ε, δ)-DP guarantee, does not state the privacy model (local DP vs. central DP), and does not explain what the guarantee means for a member of the training dataset in the EV charging context—specifically, what an adversary learns about an individual charging session from the published model. Connecting the formal guarantee to the application context is essential for a systems security venue and is entirely absent from the current document.

### 2.7 CaseStudies.md

CaseStudies.md is a placeholder document in its current form. Case Study 1 (CS1) has one preliminary result—AUC-ROC=0.5172 at ε=1.0, 100 rounds—but the remainder of the planned experimental sweep (rounds ∈ {100, 200, 500, 1000} × ε ∈ {0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 5.0}) is described only as "in progress." Case Studies 2 and 3 have not been initiated. A DSN submission cannot be made on the basis of a single preliminary result; the full experimental results are the core scientific content of the paper, and without them, the paper cannot be written. The document correctly identifies this gap, but it must be treated as a critical path item for the project timeline, not merely a documentation shortcoming.

The results section should be redesigned to anticipate the full structure of the experimental report, including: per-experiment AUC-ROC and confidence intervals, per-cluster breakdown, sensitivity analysis across ε values, and comparison against the IDS baseline. The document should include planned analysis methodologies, such as the statistical tests that will be used to assess whether differences across ε values are significant (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank test for AUC-ROC comparison, given that the distribution of AUC-ROC scores across repeated runs may not be normal). Anticipating this structure in the documentation will accelerate the paper writing process once results are available.

### 2.8 DeveloperGuide.md

DeveloperGuide.md suffers from two interrelated problems: content duplication and a failure of narrative register. The duplicated content—which overlaps with sections of Architecture.md—should be eliminated, with cross-references substituted. More fundamentally, a DeveloperGuide for a research framework serves a different purpose than an API reference: it should narrate the extension points of the framework with enough motivation that a researcher from another group could instantiate the framework on a different dataset, protocol stack, or FL algorithm without deep familiarity with the original implementation. The current document reads as an API reference for project insiders, not as a research infrastructure guide for the community.

The extension point motivation is the key missing element. When a developer wishes to add a new protocol adapter—say, for OCPP 2.0.1 or for the ISO 15118-20 Vehicle-to-Grid protocol—the guide should explain not only what interface to implement but why the interface is designed as it is and what invariants the implementation must preserve to ensure that the experimental results remain valid. Without this motivation, a developer who implements the interface incorrectly may introduce subtle artifacts into the experimental pipeline without realizing it. Similarly, the guide should explain what invariants must hold for a new MIA variant to be substituted for FedMIA, or for a new FL algorithm to replace FedAvg, and how to validate that the substitution preserves the experimental comparability that the framework is designed to guarantee.

### 2.9 Testing.md

Testing.md documents 77 unit tests across the core components and provides the component-level coverage breakdown at a coarse level, but it is missing three elements that a research framework documentation must include: quantitative coverage metrics at the line or branch level, an integration test strategy, and an end-to-end test plan. Unit tests verify individual components in isolation; they do not verify that the components interact correctly under realistic experimental conditions. For ChargeShield-FL, the most critical integration concern is the correctness of the gradient flow from local training through DP noise addition through NVFLARE aggregation to the global model update. A test that follows a gradient tensor through this entire pipeline under known inputs and verifies the output model parameters is essential for scientific validity and entirely absent from the current test suite.

The end-to-end test plan should specify at minimum one complete experiment run—ideally the CS1 baseline at 100 rounds, ε=1.0—as an automated regression test that verifies the AUC-ROC result falls within a statistically expected range. This is standard practice in ML systems research and is particularly important for a framework that will be used by other researchers after publication. The absence of such a test means that a future contributor who modifies the DP implementation cannot verify that they have not inadvertently changed the experimental results. The document should also state what percentage of the codebase is covered by the existing 77 unit tests, measured at both line and branch level, using a standard coverage tool such as pytest-cov.

### 2.10 Roadmap.md

Roadmap.md is too brief to serve as a planning document for a project targeting a specific conference deadline. It does not include a DSN 2027 submission timeline, does not identify the dependencies between tasks (e.g., the experimental sweep must be complete before the paper results section can be written, which must be complete before the camera-ready deadline), and does not assign responsibility for each task. A proper roadmap for a research project should include a dependency graph of major milestones, with estimated durations and critical path identification. Without this structure, the roadmap cannot support project management and cannot be used to assess whether the DSN 2027 deadline is achievable.

### 2.11 README.md

README.md is the face of the research artifact, the first document that a reviewer, reader, or external researcher will encounter. In its current state, it contains no abstract, no summary of results, and no positioning of the work relative to the state of the art. For a research artifact submitted alongside a DSN paper, the README must function as a condensed research statement: it should state the research question, the experimental methodology, the key results, and the instructions for reproducing those results. The ACM and IEEE artifact evaluation guidelines are explicit on this point. The README must be completely rewritten with this function in mind.

## 3. Strengths of the Framework

### 3.1 Real Dataset: ACN-Data JPL

The decision to ground ChargeShield-FL in real-world charging session data from the ACN-Data JPL site is one of the most consequential design choices in the entire framework. The dataset comprises 13,073 EV charging sessions collected during 2019 and 2020 at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory campus in Pasadena, California. Unlike synthetically generated charging profiles—which dominate the FL privacy literature in the IoT domain due to their convenience—the JPL dataset captures the actual statistical structure of EV charging demand, including the highly skewed session duration distributions, the temporal clustering of arrivals during morning commute hours, and the heterogeneity in energy delivered that reflects the diversity of EV battery capacities in the real-world fleet. This statistical realism is not cosmetic: it directly affects the reconstruction error distribution of the autoencoder, which is the signal that FedMIA uses to distinguish members from non-members. A framework validated on synthetic data would not support claims about the difficulty of MIA in real EV charging deployments; one validated on ACN-Data JPL does.

### 3.2 Realistic OT Topology

The network topology instantiated via Containerlab and Docker represents a meaningful step beyond the typical FL simulation environment. The twelve-node, four-cluster architecture—with mTLS securing inter-node communications and WireGuard providing VPN-layer isolation between clusters—captures several properties of real OT network architectures that are invisible in simulation: protocol heterogeneity, latency asymmetry between clusters (Highway DC fast chargers vs. Residential AC chargers operate on different backhaul network types), and the security zoning implicit in the Purdue Model hierarchy. OrbStack provides the macOS-native container runtime that makes the topology reproducible on developer machines without dedicated hardware, which is a pragmatic contribution to reproducibility. The emulated topology does not replace real hardware validation, but it substantially raises the realism bar above what is conventional in the FL security literature.

### 3.3 NVFLARE 2.7.2

The use of NVFLARE 2.7.2 as the FL orchestration layer distinguishes ChargeShield-FL from the large body of FL privacy research that uses custom or toy FL implementations. NVFLARE is a production-grade federated learning framework developed by NVIDIA, with active industry deployment and a well-documented security model. Its use here means that the experimental results are obtained under a realistic FL protocol implementation, including correct handling of client dropout, round timeout, and model versioning—none of which are typically addressed in custom FL implementations used in academic research. The use of a production framework also means that any privacy vulnerabilities discovered in ChargeShield-FL's experimental setup are more likely to reflect vulnerabilities in real-world deployments, rather than artifacts of a simplified implementation.

### 3.4 ML Plane Abstraction

The ML Plane is the most conceptually novel element of the ChargeShield-FL architecture. By defining a transversal logical layer that crosses the Purdue Model from Level 0 (field devices, i.e., the physical charging hardware) through Level 3 (site operations), the framework provides a principled way to instrument FL training observation without modifying the FL protocol itself. The observer pattern that underpins the ML Plane allows experimental instrumentation—gradient recording, privacy budget tracking, anomaly detection signal collection—to be attached to and detached from the running system without affecting the learning process. This property is scientifically valuable because it means that the act of measurement does not perturb the phenomenon being measured, which is a fundamental requirement for valid experimental methodology. The ML Plane represents a genuine architectural contribution that, if properly documented and motivated, could itself be a significant component of the DSN submission's contribution narrative.

### 3.5 Differential Privacy Implementation

The Gaussian Mechanism implemented in GradientManager—with the exact formula σ = max_grad_norm × √(2×ln(1.25/δ)) / ε and per-sample gradient clipping—is correct with respect to the (ε, δ)-DP definition of Dwork et al. (2006). The configurability of σ via the ε and δ parameters, combined with the gradient clipping that bounds the sensitivity of the gradient function, means that the privacy guarantee is formally grounded, not merely heuristic. This distinguishes ChargeShield-FL from a substantial body of FL privacy research in which the DP mechanism is implemented incorrectly or without formal justification. The correctness of the DP implementation is a prerequisite for the validity of all experimental claims about the privacy-utility tradeoff, and it is a genuine strength that should be highlighted prominently in the paper.

### 3.6 Clean Adapter Pattern

The adapter pattern used to abstract protocol differences in ProtocolAdapters enables the framework to run the same experimental pipeline—autoencoder training, FedMIA attack, IDS evaluation—across nodes running OCPP 1.6, OCPP 2.0.1, and MQTT v5, without modifying the core ML components. This design choice is what makes the framework genuinely extensible: adding support for a new protocol requires only the implementation of the adapter interface, not modification of the experimental logic. For a research framework intended for community adoption and artifact evaluation, this extensibility is essential. It also means that the framework can be updated as the OCPP standard evolves—OCPP 2.1 is already in development—without obsoleting the experimental results obtained under earlier versions.

### 3.7 First Experimental Result

The AUC-ROC of 0.5172 obtained by FedMIA at ε=1.0, 100 training rounds, is a significant milestone because it demonstrates that the entire experimental pipeline—from data loading through local training, gradient noise injection, NVFLARE aggregation, shadow model training, and AUC-ROC evaluation—executes correctly end-to-end and produces a result that is interpretable within the MIA literature. An AUC-ROC of approximately 0.517 at ε=1.0 is consistent with the findings of McMahan et al. (2018) and subsequent DP-FedAvg literature, which report that a moderate ε budget in the range of 1.0 to 2.0 is sufficient to reduce MIA distinguishing advantage to near-random. This result, while preliminary, validates the framework design and provides a calibration point for the full experimental sweep.

### 3.8 Test Coverage

The 77 unit tests documented in Testing.md, while lacking quantitative line-level coverage metrics, represent a meaningful investment in implementation correctness for a research framework at Sprint 5 completion. The coverage of core components—including ACNDataset, AutoencoderTrainer, GradientManager, FedAvgAggregator, and FedMIA—ensures that the fundamental computational units of the experimental pipeline are validated at the unit level. This is above the median for research codebases in the ML security domain, where test coverage is frequently neglected entirely in favor of experimental results. The 77-test suite provides a foundation on which integration and end-to-end tests can be built.

## 4. Remaining Weaknesses

### 4.1 Single-Site Dataset

ACN-Data JPL, despite its scientific value as a real-world dataset, was collected at a single site—the Jet Propulsion Laboratory campus in Pasadena, California—and represents a single geographic and demographic context. The JPL charging population consists predominantly of employees at a federal research facility, which implies a charging pattern that is more regular, more predictable, and less heterogeneous than the population one would encounter in a public urban charging network, a highway fast-charging corridor, or a residential neighborhood with high EV penetration. The training data distribution at each federated client is therefore not representative of the full diversity of EV charging patterns that a deployed system would encounter. This limitation constrains the generalizability of the MIA results: an attack that achieves AUC-ROC of 0.5172 against the JPL distribution may perform substantially differently against a more heterogeneous distribution, and the current framework cannot distinguish between a privacy guarantee that is distribution-independent and one that is calibrated to the specific statistical properties of the JPL dataset.

### 4.2 Feature Set Limitations

The six-feature schema derived from the ACN-Data JPL dataset—which captures session-level aggregates such as energy delivered and session duration—omits several real-time operational features that are present in actual OCPP message streams: per-second voltage, current, and State of Charge (SOC) readings. These time-series features are not only more informationally rich but are also more likely to contain membership-identifying signals, because individual charging curves are highly distinctive and have been shown in the automotive cybersecurity literature to function as behavioral fingerprints. The absence of these features from the experimental setup means that the MIA results may underestimate the privacy risk in a real deployment, where an attacker with access to the full OCPP message stream—rather than only the session-level aggregates—would have substantially more discriminating information.

### 4.3 Incomplete Experimental Results

The full experimental sweep—covering 20 configuration combinations across four values of training rounds and five values of ε—is in progress at the time of this review. Without these results, the paper's contribution claims about the privacy-utility tradeoff under DP-FL in OT environments cannot be substantiated. A single data point (AUC-ROC=0.5172 at ε=1.0, 100 rounds) is insufficient to characterize the behavior of the system across the privacy budget and training duration dimensions that are the primary experimental variables. The completion of the sweep is therefore on the absolute critical path for the DSN 2027 submission.

### 4.4 Rényi DP Composition

The PrivacyAuditor component does not implement Rényi Differential Privacy (RDP) composition analysis, relying instead on a simplified epsilon estimate that does not account for the tightened bounds available through the moments accountant (Abadi et al., 2016) or the RDP framework (Mironov, 2017). The practical consequence is that the epsilon values reported by the framework are likely conservative—the actual privacy expenditure is lower than reported—but the magnitude of the gap is unknown without proper composition analysis. For a DSN submission, where the claimed privacy guarantees will be scrutinized by reviewers with expertise in DP theory, reporting a simplified epsilon without acknowledging this limitation will be challenged.

### 4.5 Absence of Client-Side Attacker Scenario

The current FedMIA implementation follows the honest-but-curious aggregator threat model, in which the attacker observes gradient updates from clients and attempts to infer membership from the global model. This is Scenario 1. The more adversarially capable Scenario 2—in which a compromised FL client attempts to infer membership in other clients' training datasets—is not yet implemented. From a DSN perspective, the client-side attacker scenario is arguably more practically relevant, because compromising a single EV charging station is a more realistic attack vector than compromising the central FL aggregator, which typically operates in a more physically secured environment. The absence of Scenario 2 limits the threat coverage of the framework.

### 4.6 Missing ElaadNL Dataset Integration

The ElaadNL dataset, collected from public charging stations in the Netherlands, would provide geographic diversity, a different charging pattern distribution (European vs. American EV fleets and infrastructure), and a different protocol context (OCPP-dominated, with a mix of 1.6 and 2.0.1). Its absence from the framework means that the generalizability claims of the paper cannot be empirically supported. Integrating ElaadNL as a second dataset would require implementing an adapter for its data schema but would substantially strengthen the external validity of the experimental results.

### 4.7 Incomplete Protocol Adapter Implementations

The OCPP 2.0.1 and MQTT v5 adapters are not fully implemented at the time of this review. This means that two of the four clusters—Corporate (OCPP 2.0.1, 50kW DC) and Residential (MQTT v5, 7kW AC)—are not contributing real protocol data to the experiments. The experimental results are therefore currently derived from a subset of the intended topology, which limits the claim that the framework evaluates MIA in a heterogeneous protocol environment. The full adapter implementations must be completed and validated before the experimental sweep results can be attributed to the complete four-cluster topology.

### 4.8 No Real Hardware Validation

The entire experimental platform runs in containerized emulation via Containerlab and Docker. While this enables reproducibility, it means that the network latency, computational load, and timing characteristics observed in the experiments do not reflect those of actual EV charging hardware. Real OCPP implementations on embedded controllers—particularly the lower-capability units found in 7kW residential chargers—may exhibit timing behaviors that affect gradient synchronization, FL round completion, and therefore the statistical properties of the aggregated model. A real hardware validation, even on a small testbed, would substantially strengthen the claims about the framework's applicability to real deployments.

### 4.9 Missing Related Work Survey

No related work document exists in the corpus, and the existing documents do not contain embedded related work sections. This is the most consequential gap from the perspective of a DSN submission, because the novelty of the contribution cannot be established without explicit comparison to prior work. The related work section must cover at minimum: FL privacy evaluation frameworks, MIA against FL, DP-FL, Byzantine-robust FL, OT/ICS security, EV charging infrastructure security, and FL in IoT/edge environments. The absence of this section means that the paper cannot currently be positioned within the scientific literature.

## 5. Inconsistencies Found

### 5.1 Duplicated Content in DeveloperGuide.md

DeveloperGuide.md contains content that is substantially duplicated from Architecture.md, specifically in sections describing the component architecture of the ML Plane and the adapter layer. The duplication is not merely a matter of similar subject matter; the prose is repeated in ways that suggest copy-paste editing without subsequent differentiation. This creates a maintenance risk: as the implementation evolves, the two documents will diverge, and the inconsistency between them will confuse both internal developers and external researchers attempting to understand the framework. The resolution is to establish Architecture.md as the authoritative source for architectural descriptions and to replace the duplicated content in DeveloperGuide.md with cross-references, adding only developer-specific guidance (e.g., how to extend a component, what tests to run after a modification) that is not present in Architecture.md.

### 5.2 ML Plane Section Duplication Across Architecture.md and MLPlane.md

The ML Plane receives extended treatment in both Architecture.md and MLPlane.md, with neither document clearly designated as the authoritative source. The descriptions are not identical, which means they have already diverged and will continue to do so. In one document, the observer pattern is described primarily in terms of its implementation mechanics; in the other, it is described primarily in terms of its architectural role. The scientific reader cannot determine which description takes precedence, and the inconsistency undermines confidence in both. A single authoritative description must be established, with the other document containing only a summary and a pointer.

### 5.3 Epsilon Formula Inconsistency Between PrivacyAuditor.md and GradientManager

The most scientifically consequential inconsistency in the documentation corpus is the discrepancy between the epsilon estimation formula used in PrivacyAuditor.md and the exact Gaussian Mechanism formula implemented in GradientManager. GradientManager correctly computes the noise standard deviation as σ = max_grad_norm × √(2×ln(1.25/δ)) / ε, which is the standard formula for the Gaussian Mechanism that achieves (ε, δ)-DP under the condition ε < 1 (Dwork and Roth, 2014, Theorem A.1). PrivacyAuditor.md uses a simplified formula that does not include the logarithmic factor, which means that the epsilon value it reports for a given σ is not the true epsilon of the mechanism—it is an underestimate of the actual noise level relative to the claimed privacy budget, or equivalently, an overestimate of the privacy protection achieved. Any AUC-ROC result reported alongside a claimed epsilon value that was computed using the simplified formula must be treated as incorrectly characterized until this discrepancy is resolved. The PrivacyAuditor implementation must be corrected to use the exact inversion of the GradientManager formula, and any previously reported epsilon values must be recomputed.

## 6. Potential DSN Reviewer Objections

### 6.1 Objection: Single Dataset Insufficiency

*As a reviewer would phrase it:* "The authors evaluate their framework on a single dataset from a single site (ACN-Data JPL). This severely limits the generalizability of the results. How can the authors claim that their privacy evaluation framework is applicable to EV charging infrastructure broadly when all experimental evidence is drawn from one campus environment with one demographic profile?"

The objection is legitimate and the authors should acknowledge it directly rather than deflect it. The appropriate response is threefold. First, the choice of ACN-Data JPL should be explicitly justified on the grounds that it is the only publicly available dataset of real EV charging sessions at the required granularity, and that the alternative—synthetic data—would introduce its own comparability limitations. The authors should cite the data availability constraints as a known limitation of the field rather than a weakness specific to their work. Second, the framework's architecture should be presented as dataset-agnostic by design: the adapter pattern that abstracts the ACN-Data schema can accommodate any dataset that can be mapped to the six-feature schema, and the extension to ElaadNL is identified as future work with specific steps described. Third, the authors should present sensitivity analyses that characterize the dependence of the MIA results on the statistical properties of the input distribution—for example, by creating synthetic perturbations of the JPL dataset that simulate higher heterogeneity—to argue that the AUC-ROC results are not artifacts of the specific JPL distribution.

### 6.2 Objection: MIA Method Novelty

*As a reviewer would phrase it:* "The use of autoencoder reconstruction error as an MIA signal is not new. This approach has been applied in prior work on generative model privacy. The authors have not proposed a new attack; they have applied an existing technique to a new domain. Is domain application alone sufficient for DSN?"

This objection correctly identifies that the autoencoder-based MIA is not a methodological contribution in isolation. The response must reframe the contribution clearly and without equivocation: the contribution of ChargeShield-FL is the framework, not the attack algorithm. The autoencoder MIA is instantiated as one attack variant within a general framework whose scientific value lies in its ability to systematically evaluate the privacy-utility tradeoff under DP-FL in a realistic OT environment. The analogous claim would apply to any experimental infrastructure paper: the contribution of a network testbed paper is the testbed and the experimental methodology, not the specific traffic generation algorithm. The authors should draw this distinction explicitly and cite precedent from the systems security literature for framework contributions (e.g., the DeterLab testbed, the CAIDA datasets, the WIDE project) in which the infrastructure itself constitutes the scientific contribution. Additionally, the authors should note that the FedMIA shadow model approach (Shokri et al., 2017) is instantiated in the specific context of time-series anomaly detection models trained on OT data, which introduces application-specific considerations—particularly around the temporal structure of charging session data—that are not addressed in the general MIA literature.

### 6.3 Objection: AUC-ROC Near Random

*As a reviewer would phrase it:* "An AUC-ROC of 0.5172 is barely above random chance. If the DP mechanism already defeats the attack at ε=1.0, the paper has demonstrated that DP works—which is already well established. What novel finding does the framework enable?"

This is the most probing objection the authors will face and must be addressed with precision. The single data point at ε=1.0 does not tell the complete story; it is precisely one point in the full sweep across ε ∈ {0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 5.0}. The scientifically interesting question is not whether DP defeats MIA at a moderate ε—it does, and this is known—but rather where the phase transition occurs in the privacy-utility tradeoff for EV charging data specifically. At ε=5.0 or ε=10.0, the DP noise is insufficient to reduce MIA to random, and the attack succeeds. The location of the transition point, the shape of the AUC-ROC curve as a function of ε and round count, and the interaction between these variables in the OT/EV context are the novel empirical contributions. Furthermore, the AUC-ROC metric should be supplemented with a model utility metric—such as reconstruction MSE on held-out data—to characterize the privacy-utility frontier, which is the quantity of direct practical relevance to infrastructure operators deciding on a DP budget. The framework's value lies precisely in its ability to generate this frontier under realistic conditions.

### 6.4 Objection: Weak Adversarial Model

*As a reviewer would phrase it:* "The honest-but-curious assumption for the aggregator is one of the weakest possible threat models in FL security. A realistic attacker controlling the aggregator could do far more than passive inference; they could conduct active model poisoning or gradient inversion attacks. Why has the paper chosen such a conservative threat model?"

The honest-but-curious aggregator is the appropriate starting point for an MIA evaluation because it isolates the membership inference question from the model poisoning question, which is a separate attack class. The response should acknowledge that the threat model is deliberately scoped to passive inference—because this is the threat that DP is designed to address—and that active attacks are a complementary but distinct threat category. The authors should cite the relevant literature on gradient inversion attacks (Zhu et al., 2019; Geiping et al., 2020) to demonstrate awareness of the broader threat landscape and describe Scenario 2 (client-side attacker) as the next threat model to be implemented, which broadens the coverage beyond the aggregator-only assumption. The key argument is that a framework contribution gains its value from the ability to evaluate multiple threat models incrementally; the honest-but-curious aggregator is the first instantiation, not the final one. The paper should make this framing explicit in its threat model section and structure the case studies accordingly.

### 6.5 Objection: IDS Mechanism Novelty

*As a reviewer would phrase it:* "CUSUM, Krum, and Cosine Similarity are standard techniques that appear in the FL Byzantine robustness literature from 2017 onward. The paper has not introduced any new detection mechanism. What is the contribution of the IDS layer?"

The response to this objection parallels the response to the MIA novelty objection: the IDS components are detection baselines, not contributions. Their role in the framework is to provide a comparative evaluation layer that answers the question of whether standard anomaly detection can identify FL clients that are under MIA attack—or, equivalently, whether a membership inference attack can evade standard detection mechanisms. This is a well-defined and answerable research question whose answer is not obvious: CUSUM is sensitive to distributional shifts in gradient norms; an MIA attacker who calibrates their shadow model to produce gradients whose norms are within the normal range could in principle evade CUSUM detection. Whether this evasion is achievable in the EV charging context is an empirical question, and ChargeShield-FL is structured to answer it. The authors should frame the IDS layer explicitly as a baseline evaluation layer and present the detection results alongside the MIA results in the experimental section, making clear that the comparison is between the attack's evasion capability and the baseline detection sensitivity.

### 6.6 Objection: Comparison with Existing Frameworks

*As a reviewer would phrase it:* "There are existing FL privacy evaluation frameworks, including PySyft, Flower with privacy extensions, and FATE. What does ChargeShield-FL provide that these do not? The paper must demonstrate that existing tools are insufficient for the evaluation it performs."

This objection requires a careful comparative analysis that is currently absent from the documentation. The key differentiators of ChargeShield-FL are: (1) the use of a production FL orchestrator (NVFLARE) rather than a research framework, (2) the emulation of a realistic OT network topology with protocol heterogeneity, (3) the integration of real EV charging data rather than standard ML benchmark datasets, and (4) the ML Plane abstraction that enables cross-layer observation without protocol modification. PySyft, Flower, and FATE are general-purpose FL frameworks that do not model OT network topologies, do not support protocol heterogeneity at the transport layer, and do not provide the OT-specific threat model that ChargeShield-FL embeds. The authors should write a specific comparison table or prose comparison for the paper that demonstrates these distinctions with concrete technical evidence and situates ChargeShield-FL as the first framework to combine all four properties in a single reproducible platform.

### 6.7 Objection: Emulation Realism

*As a reviewer would phrase it:* "The entire experimental platform is containerized. Container networking does not faithfully reproduce the latency, jitter, and packet loss characteristics of real industrial networks. How can claims about FL behavior in EV charging infrastructure be supported by experiments run in an emulated environment?"

This objection should be addressed through two arguments. First, the paper's primary claims are about the privacy properties of the FL algorithm under DP, not about network performance. MIA AUC-ROC is determined by the statistical properties of the gradient distributions, which are a function of the data distribution and the training algorithm, not of the network transport layer. Network emulation faithfully reproduces the logical behavior of the FL protocol—client selection, gradient aggregation, model broadcast—and the privacy guarantees of the Gaussian Mechanism are provably independent of the network topology. Second, the Containerlab emulation does introduce realistic latency profiles through traffic shaping, and the WireGuard VPN layer introduces encryption overhead that is representative of real OT network conditions. The authors should quantify these emulation parameters and demonstrate that they are within the range of real EV charging backhaul network characteristics, citing real-world network measurement studies where available. An optional real hardware validation on even two nodes would convert this potential weakness into a demonstration of robustness.

## 7. Literature Review Gaps

### 7.1 Membership Inference Attacks Against Federated Learning

The foundational MIA literature must be covered with precision. Shokri et al. (2017) introduced the shadow model approach—the method used by FedMIA—and must be cited as the primary methodological reference for the attack. Nasr et al. (2019) extended MIA to the white-box federated setting and demonstrated that gradient access provides substantially more information to the attacker than black-box model access; this paper is directly relevant to the threat model formulation and to the decision to use gradient-based MIA as the primary attack vector. Carlini et al. (2022) provided a rigorous quantitative framework for evaluating MIA and argued that AUC-ROC may understate membership inference risk when the attacker focuses on the most vulnerable records—the canary insertion approach—which bears directly on the interpretation of the AUC-ROC=0.5172 result and suggests that per-record vulnerability analysis should be included in the experimental design. Hu et al. (2022) specifically studied MIA in the federated learning setting with non-IID data, which is highly relevant to the EV charging context where different clusters have qualitatively different charging patterns. The absence of any of these papers from the related work would be immediately noticed by a DSN reviewer with FL privacy expertise.

### 7.2 Differential Privacy in Federated Learning

McMahan et al. (2018) is the canonical reference for DP-FedAvg and must be cited extensively in the PrivacyAuditor and GradientManager documentation, as well as in the paper. The moments accountant introduced in that work is the composition technique that the PrivacyAuditor should implement, and the epsilon values reported in the paper should be compared to those reported by McMahan et al. to establish calibration. Geyer et al. (2017) introduced client-level DP for FL, which provides a different privacy guarantee—protecting the participation of an entire client rather than individual records—and is relevant to the threat model discussion because it raises the question of whether ChargeShield-FL protects individual sessions or individual charging stations. Abadi et al. (2016) introduced the moments accountant for tight composition analysis; Mironov (2017) formalized Rényi Differential Privacy as a more analytically tractable alternative. Without these references, the DP treatment in the paper will appear underdeveloped to specialists.

### 7.3 Byzantine-Robust Federated Learning

Blanchard et al. (2017) introduced the Krum aggregation rule—one of the three IDS baseline mechanisms—and must be cited in IDS.md and in the paper with the specific claim that Krum is used as a detection baseline. Yin et al. (2018) provided convergence analysis for coordinate-wise median and trimmed mean aggregation, which provides theoretical grounding for understanding why gradient-norm-based anomaly detection works in some settings and establishes the formal relationship between Byzantine robustness and DP noise. Cao and Fang (2020) introduced FLTrust, a trust score-based aggregation method that outperforms Krum in several attack scenarios; its omission from the baseline comparison should be explicitly acknowledged and justified, or FLTrust should be added as a fourth IDS mechanism. The Byzantine robustness literature must be covered not only as context for the IDS components but also because the interaction between Byzantine robustness mechanisms and DP noise is non-trivial: adding Gaussian noise changes the gradient distribution in ways that can affect Byzantine detection rates, a confound that the experiments should explicitly control for.

### 7.4 Privacy in OT and ICS Systems

IEC 62443 is the primary international standard for industrial cybersecurity and provides the normative framework within which any OT security contribution must be situated. Specifically, IEC 62443-3-3 defines system security requirements and security levels; the ChargeShield-FL threat model should map its adversary capabilities to the IEC 62443 Security Level taxonomy. This mapping would allow infrastructure operators to directly translate the framework's experimental findings into compliance-relevant terms. ETSI EN 303 645 provides cybersecurity requirements for consumer IoT, which is relevant to the Residential cluster nodes that operate on MQTT v5 with 7kW AC chargers, a profile that is closer to consumer IoT than to industrial OT. Without grounding the threat model in these standards, the paper's OT security claims will appear disconnected from the regulatory and standards context that practitioners and domain-expert reviewers expect.

### 7.5 EV Infrastructure Security

ISO 15118 is the international standard for Vehicle-to-Grid communication, including the Plug & Charge protocol and the associated PKI infrastructure. The OCPP Security Whitepaper from the Open Charge Alliance provides security requirements for OCPP implementations, including recommendations for TLS certificate management that are directly relevant to the mTLS configuration in ChargeShield-FL. These references are essential for grounding the protocol choices and security architecture in the domain standards. A DSN reviewer with EV infrastructure expertise will expect to see ISO 15118 cited in any paper that makes security claims about OCPP-based charging networks, and its absence will suggest insufficient domain knowledge on the part of the authors.

### 7.6 Federated Learning in IoT and Edge Environments

The non-IID data challenge in federated learning—where the local data distributions at different clients differ substantially from the global distribution—is directly relevant to the EV charging setting, where Highway, Urban, Residential, and Corporate clusters have qualitatively different charging patterns. Li et al. (2020) introduced FedProx, one of the two FL algorithms implemented in ChargeShield-FL, specifically to address the convergence challenges caused by non-IID data; this paper must be cited with the specific claim that FedProx is included because the EV charging data is expected to be non-IID across clusters. Zhao et al. (2018) provided an empirical characterization of the accuracy degradation caused by non-IID data in FedAvg; the results of this paper provide the theoretical motivation for including FedProx as an experimental variant and for expecting different AUC-ROC behaviors across clusters. The non-IID nature of the data is also directly relevant to the MIA analysis, since non-IID distributions make the shadow model assumption (that the attacker knows the global distribution) harder to satisfy.

### 7.7 Threat Modeling Methodologies

The STRIDE methodology (Shostack, 2014) is the standard threat modeling framework for systems security and should be used to structure the ThreatModel.md document. A STRIDE decomposition of the ChargeShield-FL threat surface would systematically enumerate all six threat categories across the relevant system components—the FL clients, the aggregator, the ML Plane observation bus, and the network transport layer—and ensure that no threat class is overlooked. PASTA (Ucedavelez and Morana, 2015) provides a risk-centric complement to STRIDE that is more appropriate for infrastructure deployments where business risk quantification is required alongside technical threat enumeration. For ICS-specific threat modeling, the MITRE ATT&CK for ICS framework provides a taxonomy of adversary tactics and techniques specifically developed for operational technology environments and should be used to ensure that the ChargeShield-FL threat model covers the relevant ICS attack surface, including techniques such as remote access trojans targeting SCADA systems and lateral movement within OT networks.

## 8. Component Maturity Assessment

### 8.1 ACNDataset Adapter

**Maturity: Research-Grade.** The ACNDataset adapter successfully parses the JPL dataset and maps charging session records to the six-feature schema used by the AutoencoderTrainer. It is covered by unit tests and produces the correct output for the 13,073-session corpus. However, it is not generalized to other dataset schemas—ElaadNL integration would require a separate adapter—and does not handle missing values, sensor faults, or session anomalies in the raw data in a principled way. The adapter is fit for the current experimental purpose but not yet suitable for deployment in a pipeline that must handle arbitrary real-world EV charging data. Its maturity is appropriate to the current sprint status, and it is not a blocking concern for the DSN submission provided the ElaadNL dataset integration is deferred to future work.

### 8.2 AutoencoderTrainer

**Maturity: Research-Grade.** The 6→16→8→4 encoder-decoder architecture implemented in PyTorch with MSE loss is a standard anomaly detection autoencoder and is correctly implemented for the six-feature input schema. The training loop, loss monitoring, and model serialization are covered by unit tests. The architecture is not yet validated on out-of-distribution data (e.g., ElaadNL sessions) and does not implement early stopping or learning rate scheduling, which may affect convergence behavior over long training runs (1000 rounds). The component is suitable for the current experiments but would need hyperparameter search and cross-validation before claims about optimal architecture could be made. The choice of 6→16→8→4 is not theoretically motivated in the documentation and should be justified, at minimum by a brief ablation study.

### 8.3 GradientManager (DP)

**Maturity: Research-Grade, approaching Engineering-Grade.** The GradientManager implements the Gaussian Mechanism correctly, with per-sample gradient clipping and configurable (ε, δ) parameters. The formula σ = max_grad_norm × √(2×ln(1.25/δ)) / ε is mathematically correct for the standard Gaussian Mechanism under the condition ε < 1 (Dwork and Roth, 2014). The component is covered by unit tests that verify the noise magnitude and clipping behavior. The remaining gap to Engineering-Grade is the absence of RDP composition tracking, which would be required for a production DP implementation that must provide accountable cumulative privacy budgets across long training runs. This is the highest-priority component improvement from a scientific validity standpoint, pending the resolution of the PrivacyAuditor inconsistency.

### 8.4 FedAvgAggregator

**Maturity: Engineering-Grade.** The FedAvgAggregator wraps the NVFLARE 2.7.2 aggregation interface with the standard weighted average aggregation rule of McMahan et al. (2017). NVFLARE's production implementation handles client dropout, round timeout, and model versioning correctly, and the aggregator inherits these properties. The component is covered by unit tests and integrates correctly with the GradientManager DP pipeline. It is the most mature component in the framework, benefiting from NVFLARE's engineering-grade implementation as its foundation. The remaining gap is that the FedProx variant (Li et al., 2020) must be validated to converge correctly under the non-IID EV charging data distribution before its results can be compared to FedAvg results.

### 8.5 PrivacyAuditor

**Maturity: Research-Grade (with a known correctness defect).** As documented in Section 5.3, the epsilon estimation formula in PrivacyAuditor does not match the exact Gaussian Mechanism formula used in GradientManager. Until this inconsistency is resolved, the PrivacyAuditor cannot be used to report epsilon values in a published paper. Beyond the formula defect, the auditor lacks RDP composition analysis, making its reported cumulative epsilon values unreliable for long training runs. This component requires the most immediate attention of any component in the framework. It is the only component with a known correctness defect that directly affects the validity of reported experimental results.

### 8.6 FedMIA

**Maturity: Research-Grade.** The FedMIA implementation of the Shokri et al. (2017) shadow model approach is correctly instantiated for the autoencoder setting: shadow models are trained on data samples drawn from the same distribution as the target model's training data, and membership is inferred from the reconstruction error distribution. The AUC-ROC evaluation pipeline produces results consistent with the theoretical expectations from the DP-FL literature. The component is covered by unit tests. Its limitation is that it implements only the black-box, aggregator-side attack scenario; the client-side attacker scenario (Scenario 2) is not yet implemented. The component is mature enough for the CS1 experiments but will need extension before CS2 can be run.

### 8.7 ChargingIDS (CUSUM/Krum/Cosine Similarity)

**Maturity: Research-Grade (as a detection baseline layer).** Each of the three IDS mechanisms is correctly implemented relative to its defining literature: CUSUM implements the standard cumulative sum control chart, Krum implements the Blanchard et al. (2017) aggregation rule, and Cosine Similarity measures the angular deviation between local gradient vectors. The components are covered by unit tests. Their maturity as research-grade baselines is adequate for their role in the framework; they are not intended as production-ready intrusion detection systems. The primary documentation gap—their framing as features rather than baselines—is a documentation concern, not a component maturity concern. The implementations themselves are correct.

### 8.8 Containerlab Topology

**Maturity: Engineering-Grade.** The twelve-node, four-cluster Containerlab topology with mTLS and WireGuard is a well-configured network emulation environment that is reproducible on developer machines via OrbStack. The topology definition files are version-controlled and the mTLS certificates are managed within the project. The primary limitation—containerized emulation rather than real hardware—is a deliberate design choice for reproducibility and is appropriate for the research objectives. The topology is mature for its intended purpose, and the emulation fidelity is sufficient to reproduce the logical FL protocol behavior that is the primary experimental variable.

### 8.9 NVFLARE Integration

**Maturity: Engineering-Grade.** The NVFLARE 2.7.2 integration is well-configured, with correct client and server job definitions, model sharing configuration, and round management. The integration inherits NVFLARE's production-grade handling of client lifecycle management, model versioning, and secure communication. The component is covered by integration tests within the NVFLARE testing framework. The remaining gap is that the NVFLARE configuration has not been tested under adversarial network conditions—packet loss, latency injection, client dropout under load—that might affect round completion rates in a real OT network. This gap is acceptable for a first DSN submission but should be addressed in the real hardware validation work.

### 8.10 ML Plane Abstraction

**Maturity: Research-Grade.** The ML Plane is the most novel architectural contribution in the framework and the least mature in terms of documentation and formalization. The observer pattern implementation is functional—gradient observations are correctly recorded and routed across the Purdue Model levels—but the abstraction boundary is not formally specified and the extension contract for adding new observation points is not documented. The component is mature enough to support the current experiments but not yet mature enough to be presented as a reusable abstraction for external researchers without substantially improved documentation and interface specification. Raising the ML Plane from Research-Grade to Engineering-Grade requires: a formal interface specification, documented invariants, extension examples, and validation tests that verify the observer pattern does not perturb the FL training process.

## 9. Priority Action Items

The following items are ranked in strict priority order based on their impact on DSN 2027 submission readiness. Items ranked higher have either blocking dependencies for downstream tasks or directly gate the scientific validity of the submission.

**1. Complete the full experimental sweep (rounds × ε).** The sweep covering rounds ∈ {100, 200, 500, 1000} × ε ∈ {0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 5.0} is the primary source of experimental results for the paper. Without these 20 data points—plus per-cluster breakdowns and repeated runs for confidence interval estimation—the paper has no main results section. This item is on the critical path for all downstream paper-writing tasks and must be prioritized above all others except the PrivacyAuditor fix, which must precede result reporting.

**2. Resolve the PrivacyAuditor epsilon formula inconsistency.** The discrepancy between the simplified epsilon estimate in PrivacyAuditor.md and the exact Gaussian Mechanism formula in GradientManager must be resolved before any epsilon values are reported in any publication. This is a correctness defect with direct scientific consequences. The corrected formula must use the exact inversion of σ = max_grad_norm × √(2×ln(1.25/δ)) / ε, and all previously recorded epsilon values must be recomputed under the corrected formula.

**3. Write the related work survey.** The absence of a related work section is the single most serious documentation gap from a DSN submission perspective. A rigorous related work section must cover: MIA against FL (Shokri 2017, Nasr 2019, Carlini 2022, Hu 2022), DP-FL (McMahan 2018, Geyer 2017, Abadi 2016, Mironov 2017), Byzantine-robust FL (Blanchard 2017, Yin 2018, Cao 2020), OT/ICS security (IEC 62443, ETSI EN 303 645), EV infrastructure (ISO 15118, OCPP Security Whitepaper), and FL in IoT/edge (Li 2020 FedProx, Zhao 2018 non-IID). This task does not depend on experimental results and should proceed in parallel with the sweep.

**4. Conduct rigorous statistical analysis of experimental results.** Once the sweep is complete, the results must be subjected to: confidence interval estimation for AUC-ROC across repeated runs, Wilcoxon signed-rank tests for pairwise ε comparisons, per-cluster breakdown analysis, and privacy-utility frontier plotting (AUC-ROC vs. reconstruction MSE as a function of ε). This analysis constitutes the core of the experimental section and must be completed before the paper draft can begin.

**5. Implement and execute Case Studies 2 and 3.** CS2 (client-side attacker scenario) and CS3 (multi-cluster MIA with non-IID data analysis) must be designed, implemented, and executed. CS2 is particularly critical for reviewer credibility because it addresses the honest-but-curious aggregator objection and demonstrates that the framework is not confined to the weakest adversarial model. The results of CS2 and CS3 will determine the breadth of the threat coverage claims in the paper.

**6. Formalize the threat model with STRIDE alignment.** ThreatModel.md must be rewritten to include a formal attacker capability model with explicit observability and control assumptions, explicit trust assumptions per system component, STRIDE alignment across all identified threats, and an attacker knowledge model that distinguishes black-box and white-box cases. This work should be parallelized with the experimental sweep.

**7. Implement Rényi DP composition analysis in PrivacyAuditor.** Once the formula inconsistency is resolved, the PrivacyAuditor should be extended to implement RDP composition tracking using the Mironov (2017) framework or the moments accountant of Abadi et al. (2016). This will make the reported cumulative epsilon values tighter and more accurate over long training runs, aligning the framework with the current state of the art in DP composition analysis and making the privacy claims directly comparable to those in the McMahan et al. (2018) paper.

**8. Write the paper draft.** Once items 1 through 6 are substantially complete, the paper draft should be written in the DSN 2027 format. The contribution narrative should foreground: (a) the ChargeShield-FL framework as a novel research infrastructure for MIA evaluation in OT environments; (b) the ML Plane as a novel transversal architectural abstraction for FL observation across Purdue Model levels; and (c) the empirical characterization of the MIA privacy-utility tradeoff under DP-FedAvg and DP-FedProx in a realistic heterogeneous EV charging deployment. The paper should not be written until items 1 through 6 are substantially complete, as partial results will force rewrites.

**9. Integrate a second dataset (ElaadNL).** The integration of ElaadNL as a second dataset would address the single-site generalizability objection and substantially strengthen the paper's external validity claims. This item is not blocking for a first DSN submission but would materially improve the paper's reception. The ElaadNL adapter should be implemented in parallel with the paper writing process, with the results presented as a generalizability analysis or a supplementary case study.

**10. Real hardware validation (optional but recommended).** A small-scale validation of the framework on real OCPP-capable charging hardware—even a single Highway-class and a single Residential-class node—would address the emulation realism objection and transform a potential reviewer concern into a demonstrated strength. This is the most resource-intensive item on the list and is marked optional for the DSN 2027 submission, but it would be a significant differentiator if feasible within the project timeline and is strongly recommended if laboratory hardware is accessible.
